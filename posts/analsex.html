I find sodomy to be a tremendous pain in the ass. That is, the term not the act. It is written in the Hebrew bible -and thus plagarized in many others- that "Sodomy" refers to the story of Sodom (Genesis 19), where the sin of the city was traditionally interpreted as inhospitality, violence, and sexual exploitation (not exclusively homosexual acts). Early Jewish and Christian commentary often emphasized broader moral corruption rather than hyper-fixating on the same-sex behavior that occured.

In fact, it was not until Medieval & Later Christian Interpretation that the term became increasingly associated with non-procreative sexual acts, especially anal sex (whether same-sex or heterosexual). Over time, it narrowed further to primarily denote male homosexual acts due to cultural and legal stigmatization. Today, "sodomy" is almost exclusively conflated with anal sex (especially between men), and in legal contexts, it has been used to criminalize homosexuality.


This is a classic example of manufactured pejoration. Where standard pejoration is the natural drift of a word's meaning toward the negative, the manufactured variety is a deliberate, bad-faith redefinition engineered to serve an agenda (in this case Puritanism). It is, in effect, "definitional gerrymandering"—or, to put it more bluntly, the outright weaponization of language.


This concept, of course, is far from new. George Orwell's seminal novel, 1984, examined this phenomenon with such clarity that it gave us the term "Orwellian" to describe manipulative and deceptive language. The ultimate irony is that Orwell himself became an example of this metamorphosis, as his name was redefined to represent the very tool of propaganda he warned the world against!


Which brings me to the subject of today's post: Zionism. A word which -amongst goyim- has reached a level of cognative dissonence and coloquial hysteria so potent that its meaning has splintered into little more than an opaque slur.

After millennia of persecution and with no viable refuge, the Jewish people, armed with 4,000 years of firsthand experience overcoming competitive antisemitism, decided enough was enough. The only path to salvation was self-determination in their ancestral homeland—a land where, contrary to popular belief, a continuous Jewish presence has persisted for millennia, despite vast and unimaginable persecution.


Zionism is NOT:


  • White: Let's get this straight—Jews come in all shapes, sizes, and shades. The perception of Jews as uniformly "white" is a myopic Western view that conveniently ignores the rich tapestry of Jewish ethnic diversity. We have Ashkenazi Jews from Eastern Europe (though being Ashkenazi doesn't necessarily mean you're white), Sephardi Jews from Spain and Portugal, Mizrahi Jews from the Middle East and North Africa, Beta Israel from Ethiopia, and countless other communities spanning the globe.

    This misconception persists precisely because of racism. The most visible Jewish immigrants to the West over the past century were often white-passing Ashkenazim, creating a selection bias that cemented the image of the "Woody Allen" or "George Soros" Jew in the popular imagination. This conveniently erased the reality of a global diaspora with nowhere to turn but their ancestral homeland.

    Critically, these distinct communities all stem from the same origin: the ancient Israelites. Their diversity is not a product of mass conversion, but of 2,000 years of diaspora—a story of communities adapting to host cultures across the globe while tenaciously preserving their unique identity, often at great peril.

    The ultimate irony is found in the stories of those Jews who defy the "white" caricature. Consider the Mizrahi Jews, indigenous to the Middle East, who were ethnically cleansed from Arab and Persian lands en masse after 1948. Or the black Beta Israel of Ethiopia, who were airlifted to safety in dramatic rescues. To claim that their return to Zion is a "white colonial" project is not only ahistorical—it is a profound insult, leveraging the very racism that made their exodus necessary in the first place.

  • Colonialism: This accusation inverts the historical reality. Are migrants from Mexico crossing into the southwestern United States considered "colonizers"? Of course not; they are understood as people moving within a region their cultures have inhabited for millennia. The same framework applies to Zionism. Classic European colonialism is defined by a foreign empire extracting wealth and imposing foreign rule on a distant, indigenous population. Zionism represents the opposite: the return of a globally dispersed indigenous people to their historic homeland, from which they were never fully absent. They did not arrive to serve a distant empire, but to rebuild their own. When Jews returned, they weren't planting a foreign flag—they were reviving a Hebrew-speaking society in the very land where their language, culture, and kingdom originated, with archaeological evidence attesting to a continuous connection that predates the existence of most modern states.

  • Racism: This conflates self-determination with supremacy. The foundational principle of Zionism is the right of the Jewish people to sovereignty, not domination over others. This is codified in Israel's Declaration of Independence, which explicitly guarantees "complete equality of social and political rights to all its inhabitants irrespective of religion, race or sex." This is not a hollow promise but a living reality for Israel's two million Arab citizens, who serve as Supreme Court justices, members of parliament, doctors, and journalists integrated into the fabric of society. The true racism lies in the deeply ingrained bigotry that a Jewish state is inherently illegitimate—the patronizing assumption that Jews are incapable of self-governance without becoming oppressors.

  • Monolithic: This is a gross oversimplification designed to dismiss a complex ideology outright. Zionism is no more monolithic than American patriotism, which can represent a Bernie Sanders supporter, a Donald Trump voter, a Texas rancher, and a Silicon Valley CEO. The Zionist tent similarly includes secular socialists who founded the kibbutzim, free-market capitalists, those who champion a two-state solution, those who vehemently oppose it, religious mystics, and staunch atheists. To reduce this entire spectrum to its most extreme elements is as intellectually dishonest as defining all American identity by its most radical fringes. This diversity of thought is not a weakness but the sign of a living, breathing, and adaptable national movement.

  • Opposition to Palestinian Rights: This is a false dichotomy. Zionism, at its core, is about securing Jewish self-determination; it is not inherently an opposition to Palestinian rights. In fact, a significant strand of Zionist thought has always supported a two-state solution, recognizing that the conflict is a tragic clash between two legitimate national movements, not a cartoonish villainy. Some of the most principled critics of Israeli policy are themselves Zionists who argue that the project of Jewish sovereignty is morally compromised without a parallel path to Palestinian statehood. The binary that forces a choice between supporting Palestinian rights or Jewish rights is the very tautological trap this essay describes—a manipulative framing that guarantees perpetual conflict.

  • A License for War Crimes: This is the ultimate conflation of policy with principle. Criticizing specific Israeli military actions or government policies is not only legitimate but necessary. However, weaponizing those criticisms to invalidate the very right of Jewish self-determination is a profound logical fallacy. It is the equivalent of discarding the principle of American statehood because of the injustices of the Iraq War, or nullifying French sovereignty over the atrocities of the Algerian conflict. Holding Israel to a high standard is just; holding it to a solitary standard of flawless conduct that no other nation on earth could meet—especially in existential conflicts—is not scrutiny, it is demonization.


The end result of this metamorphesis is what can be described as a tautological redefinition—a rhetorical trap where a term is redefined in negative terms, and this new definition is then used to automatically condemn it. The logic is circular and unassailable: "Zionism is bad because it is defined as bad."



This tautological redefinition of Zionism follows the same pattern we saw with "sodomy": take a term with a specific historical meaning, strip it of context, and weaponize it through this circular logic until the original definition becomes unrecognizable. We're left with a situation where saying "I'm a Zionist" is treated like saying "I enjoy kicking puppies"—a statement so morally repugnant that it requires no further explanation. The tautology has become self-sustaining: "Zionism is bad because it's Zionism."



This linguistic sleight-of-hand serves multiple agendas simultaneously. For antisemites, it provides a socially acceptable way to express ancient prejudices. For political activists, it creates a convenient bogeyman. And for the intellectually lazy, it offers pre-packaged moral certainty without the inconvenience of historical nuance.



The solution, as with all tautological traps, is to insist on precision. When someone says "Zionism," ask them to define their terms. Challenge the circular logic. Remind them that words have histories, and those histories matter.



Because if we allow language to be hijacked this way, we're not just losing definitions—we're losing the ability to think clearly about complex issues. And when we lose the shared language to debate our differences, we destroy the very bridge that any lasting peace must be built upon.
